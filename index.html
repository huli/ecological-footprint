The ecological footprint
of our world

The ecological footprint measures the amount of natural resources we use in one year.

If the demand we oppose to our nature exceeds the supply, the world sooner or later will not be able to recover.

Let's see, how we do.

Human development drives footprint

The further a country has developed, the higher the footprint most often is.

With a global footprint of 2.9 hectares we are using more than 1.5 times what our nature can regenerate. The world currently has a biocapacity of 1.7 hectares per capita.

Who is doing well?

Haiti, Eritrea and Burundi are doing very well for example.

Haiti has a ecological footprint of 0.6 hectares per capita and is therefore only using a third of the world populations mean.

Who is not doing well, at all

Luxembourg is the country with the highest footprint per per capita. Followed by Canada and Australia.

Luxembourg has a footprint of enormous 13 hectares per capita and is therefore using more than 21 times the natural ressources of Haiti.

How has the global footprint
changed over time

Our global footprint is constantly growing while the biocapacity of our planet is decreasing.

Between 1961 and the year 2013 the footprint has grown from 2.27 hectares (PC) to 2.9 while the biocapacity has shrunk from 3.1 to worrying 1.7

Which country has made the biggest change for the better?

Cyprus, Greece and Barbados are the countries with the best tendencies.

Cyprus has reduced its ressource consumption by a third in the last years.

Who is changing too - in a not so good direction?

Timor-Leste, Djibouti and Fiji are the countries with the worst trends.
